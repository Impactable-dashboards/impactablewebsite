## Body

**Most LinkedIn Ads targeting starts with the wrong question.**

The default move is "who is our ICP," then build the broadest audience that matches it. VP and Director titles, a stack of industries, a company-size band, launch. It feels thorough. It is also exactly what every competitor in your category is doing, which is why your CPMs climb and your best accounts see four versions of the same pitch in a week.

There is a better first question: **where do we have an unfair advantage, and is anyone else fighting for it?**

That intersection is what we call an advantage pocket. Three things have to be true at once:

1. **You have a real edge there.** A proof point, a niche, a use case, an integration, an outcome you can defend better than anyone in the category.
2. **Competitors are not crowding it.** Low coverage, weak messaging, or nobody addressing that segment by name.
3. **It is reachable on LinkedIn.** Enough volume in titles, firmographics, and behaviors to actually run against.

Hit all three and you get cheaper attention, higher relevance, and a message that lands because you are the obvious answer instead of the fourth option. Miss them and you are paying premium CPMs to be a footnote in a crowded auction.

**This is why targeting is downstream of positioning.**

You cannot find an advantage pocket if you do not know two things cold: what you are actually better at, and what the competitive landscape around you really looks like. Most teams have a rough sense of the first and almost no structured view of the second. So they target broad, blend in, and blame the channel.

Knowing the landscape is not a vibe. It is a map. Who covers which segments. Who says what to whom. Where the messaging is identical across five vendors. And, most valuable, where there is open ground nobody is claiming.

### Our process, start to finish

This is exactly the work we do in the Competitor Intel + Audience Worksheet build. Here is the actual sequence.

**1. Map the competitive landscape.** We chart your closest competitors against the segments, titles, and messaging angles they cover. The output is a coverage matrix: who owns what, where everyone overlaps, and where the white space is.

**2. Find the white space.** The gaps in that matrix are the openings. Segments every competitor ignores. Pains nobody is naming. Angles that are wide open. We cross those gaps against your real differentiators to surface the pockets where you can win, not just compete.

**3. Turn pockets into audience insights.** A gap is useless until it is an audience. We translate each pocket into the signals that define it: the titles and seniorities, the firmographics, the behaviors and intent signals, the companies that fit.

**4. Build the audience targeting worksheet.** Everything lands in a worksheet you can run directly in LinkedIn Campaign Manager. Primary audiences, signal and lookalike sources, suppression and exclusion lists so you stop paying to re-hit the same accounts, and a sequencing plan for which pocket to open first.

That is the path from "who's our ICP" to "here are the exact audiences where we have the advantage, in priority order." Positioning to landscape to insight to a worksheet you can launch this week.

### Try the first two steps yourself

You do not need us to start. Today:

- List your five closest competitors.
- For each, write the one segment they clearly own and the one message they lead with.
- Now look for the segment nobody listed, or the message nobody is making. That blank space is a candidate pocket.
- Ask the three questions: do we have an edge there, is it uncrowded, can we reach it on LinkedIn?

If you find even one clean pocket, you have a sharper audience than most of your category is running.
