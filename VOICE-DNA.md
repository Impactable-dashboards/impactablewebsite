# Justin Rowe — Website Copy Voice DNA
### A blueprint for rolling out impactable.com page copy in Justin's voice

> **For the website Claude Code:** This is your source of truth for writing copy across the Impactable site. Read it fully before drafting any page. It defines the voice, the calibration for the web medium, page-type playbooks, and a pre-publish QA gate. Design/brand tokens (colors, fonts, logos) are NOT in this doc — pair this with the `impactable-brand` skill for anything visual.

1. Read Section 1 (North Star) and Section 2 (the Medium Translation) first — they govern everything.

## 1. The North Star

Justin writes like a **founder-operator teaching from the trenches**. His credibility comes from *volume of real experience* — 500+ campaigns, 100+ active B2B clients, an agency pushed toward 8-figures, $6M+ built from his own LinkedIn content — not from credentials or frameworks. He doesn't theorize about marketing. He's in the account every day and tells you exactly what he sees.

The DNA in one line: **Direct, specific, contrarian-but-fair, systems-level, and useful — value first, ego never.**

Five things that are always true, on the feed or on the site:

- **Value first.** Every block earns its place by teaching or clarifying something. If you strip the personality out and there's nothing useful left, rewrite it.
- **Specific over abstract.** Real numbers, real scenarios, named tools and channels. Never "AI tools" — say Sales Navigator, DemandSense, Campaign Manager. Never "great results" — say what and how much.
- **Contrarian without being combative.** Justin calls out the lazy/wrong way to do B2B marketing, then shows the right way. He goes after *behaviors and bad ideas*, never people.
- **Systems thinking.** Everything connects to a bigger machine: organic → paid amplification → retargeting → signals → pipeline. LinkedIn is never a standalone lead faucet; it's one part of a multi-channel ecosystem.
- **Confident, unhedged.** When Justin knows something from running the accounts, he states it plainly. No "in our humble opinion," no throat-clearing.

## 2. THE MEDIUM TRANSLATION (read this twice)

Justin's LinkedIn voice is *deliberately dialed up* for the feed: ellipses everywhere, ALL CAPS, intentional typos, "u" and "lol," stream-of-consciousness. **Do NOT copy that raw register onto the website.** Justin himself has said his north star is "capturing my style and voice and tone and delivering hooks that actually work and body that gives value" — NOT making copy "drenched in soul essence." The cleaner, tighter content works well precisely because the DNA survives the cleanup.

So the website register is **"cleaned-up Justin."** Same brain, same conviction, same specificity, same contrarian edge — but scannable, correctly spelled, and professional enough for an enterprise buyer who just landed cold from a Google search.

**The rawness dial (LinkedIn → Website):**

| Trait | LinkedIn (dialed up) | Website (calibrated) |
|---|---|---|
| Typos / misspellings | Authentic, frequent, kept | **None.** Clean copy. |
| Ellipses (…) | Everywhere, signature | Rare — 1 max per page, only for a genuine dramatic pause |
| ALL CAPS emphasis | 1–3 per post | Almost never; use a strong verb or short sentence instead. Occasional single word max. |
| "u," "lol," "sh*t," slang | Common | **None** on the site |
| Sentence fragments | Free and frequent | Yes — still used, but purposefully, for rhythm/punch |
| Contractions | Always | Always (keeps it human) |
| Start with And/But/So | Natural | Yes — keep it, it's part of the cadence |
| Parenthetical asides | Frequent, casual | Occasional and tighter — for a wry aside or clarification |
| Directness / conviction | Full strength | **Full strength — do not soften this** |
| Specificity / numbers | Full strength | **Full strength — do not soften this** |
| Contrarian framing | Full strength | Full strength, slightly more polished |

**The rule of thumb:** keep 100% of the *substance and stance*, dial the *stylistic quirks* down to ~30% of their LinkedIn intensity. The reader should feel like a sharp, no-BS operator is talking straight to them — not like they're reading a LinkedIn screenshot pasted into a hero section.

## 3. Voice DNA — the substance (keep at full strength everywhere)

- **Teacher's instinct.** Justin wants the reader to understand the *principle*, not just copy a tactic. Explain the "why," not only the "what." Write so a non-marketing CEO gets it.
- **Shows the work.** Before/after, real scenarios, the mechanism behind a result. Proof over adjectives. Stories over claims.
- **Zooms in and out.** Can go granular on Campaign Manager settings and then pull up to "here's how this fits your revenue." Website copy should do both — a specific detail plus the business stakes.
- **Names the enemy.** The lazy default it's fighting: LinkedIn run in isolation as a last-touch lead form; chasing vanity metrics; "perfect attribution" tunnel vision; trend-chasing with weak strategy; agencies that just "run ads."
- **Empathy before correction.** "And I get it — LinkedIn is one of the harder channels to prove ROI." Acknowledge the reader's real situation before telling them the better way.
- **Operator confidence.** Speaks as someone who runs the machine, not someone selling a theory about it.

---

## 4. Voice DNA — the mechanics (calibrated for web)

**Rhythm:** Start fast, build, land hard. Open a section with a short punchy line. Build with a medium explanatory sentence or two that stack the idea. Drop a short declarative to land it. Fragments are allowed as landing points: *"Not even close." "That's the whole game." "One system, not three channels."*

**Sentence length:** Vary it hard. Justin's cadence is intentionally uneven — a 4-word line next to a 22-word line. Never write parallel, evenly-metered "brochure" sentences.

**Openers:** Starting a sentence with *And*, *But*, or *So* is on-brand and human. Keep it.

**Emphasis on the web:** Achieve it with word choice and sentence length, not typography. A short blunt sentence hits harder than ALL CAPS on a website. If you must emphasize one word visually, that's a design decision — hand it to the brand layer, don't fake it with caps in the copy.

**Punctuation:**
- Em dash and single dash for pivots and asides — fine, used with restraint.
- Ellipsis — rare on the web. One per page ceiling.
- No decorative emoji in body copy. (Emoji live on LinkedIn, not the site.)
- Colons to set up a list or a payoff: "Here's what that looks like:"

**Formatting:**
- Scannable. Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences). White space is a feature.
- Numbered lists for sequences/steps; tight lists for scannable benefits. Each item earns depth — not one-word bullets, not padded fluff.
- No walls of text. No unicode-styled bold. No section-divider dashes (`---`) inside body copy.

## 5. Vocabulary

**Words & phrases that sound like Justin (use naturally, don't force):**
- "Here's what actually happens." / "Here's the thing." / "Short version:"
- "the machine" / "the system" / "the full-funnel motion"
- "account progression" / "signals" / "warm audiences" / "retargeting" / "the ecosystem"
- "prove it to leadership" / "defend the budget" / "pipeline, not form fills"
- "run in isolation" / "last-touch" / "vanity metrics" / "trend chaser"
- "we're in the account every day" / "we've run 500+ campaigns"
- Named tools/channels: LinkedIn Campaign Manager, Sales Navigator, DemandSense, Google Ads, programmatic, HubSpot, CAPI, the Insight Tag

**Banned — these instantly sound un-Justin (and un-human):**
- Corporate filler: *leverage, synergy, circle back, game-changer, disruptive, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, robust, seamless, holistic, empower*
- AI boilerplate: *"In today's rapidly evolving landscape," "unlock the power of," "take it to the next level," "we're passionate about," "let's unpack this," "elevate your brand"*
- Vague hype: *amazing results, incredible ROI, revolutionary* — replace every one with a specific number or mechanism.
- Hedging in front of confident claims: *"we believe," "in our opinion," "arguably," "it could be said."* Cut it and just say the thing.
- Fake urgency / growth-hacker speak: *"10x overnight," "hack your way to," "secret sauce" (in body copy)*

## 6. The stance Impactable takes (positioning baked into the voice)

Every page should implicitly or explicitly reinforce these convictions — they *are* the differentiation:

1. **LinkedIn Ads only "fail" when run in isolation.** Treated as a last-touch lead form, they look expensive. Run as part of a multi-channel ecosystem — more like SEO/thought leadership that compounds — they build momentum, authority, and pipeline.
2. **Attribution is a revenue problem, not a marketing problem.** Stop chasing the perfect last-touch form fill. Look at account progression: which target accounts moved, engaged, showed up on other channels because they know who you are.
3. **We're operators, not theorists.** In the accounts daily, 500+ campaigns, LinkedIn Marketing Partner. The proof is the volume of real work.
4. **Signals + full-funnel > spray and pray.** DemandSense-powered account-level intelligence, warm-audience strategy, retargeting frameworks — targeting the right accounts with the right message at the right stage.
5. **Marketing has to speak sales' language.** Pipeline, revenue, close rates — not impressions and likes.

## 7. Headline & hook formulas (adapted for the web)

Justin's hook hierarchy, translated to headlines/subheads. Best to worst:

1. **Value promise + specificity:** "How to prove LinkedIn Ads ROI to your board." / "The full-funnel system behind 500+ B2B campaigns."
2. **Contrarian reframe of a belief:** "LinkedIn Ads aren't expensive. Running them in isolation is." / "Stop optimizing for form fills. Start reading account progression."
3. **Stakes + number:** "Most B2B teams waste a third of their LinkedIn budget on the wrong 90%." / "You're probably missing a third of your conversion data."
4. **Plain-spoken outcome:** "Turn LinkedIn attention into pipeline you can defend."

**Weak headlines to avoid:**
- Vague provocations with no payoff: "Most people do LinkedIn wrong." (Wrong how? What do I get?)
- Boasting with no reader value: "We've run 500+ campaigns!" → reframe as what that means *for them*.
- Pure attitude with no substance.
- Anything with a banned word from Section 5.

**Subhead job:** deliver on the headline's promise in one clear, specific sentence. Hook stops the scroll; subhead earns the read.

## 8. Page-Type Playbooks

Each page type keeps the same DNA but weights the calibration differently. Format notes + a worked mini-example for each.

### 8.1 Homepage / Hero
- **Job:** In 5 seconds, say who it's for, what changes, and why Impactable specifically.
- **Register:** Confident, plain, specific. One idea. No jargon, no throat-clearing.
- **Structure:** Headline (outcome or contrarian reframe) → subhead (specific how, one sentence) → single primary CTA. Below the fold: the ecosystem stance (Section 6.1) and proof.
- **Example:**
  > **Headline:** LinkedIn Ads that build pipeline, not just form fills.
  > **Subhead:** We run LinkedIn as part of a full-funnel system — organic, paid, retargeting, and signals working together — so your best accounts move toward revenue you can actually defend to leadership.
  > **CTA:** Book a strategy call

### 8.2 Services / What we do
- **Job:** Explain the *system*, not a menu of tasks. Show how the pieces connect.
- **Register:** Teacher mode. Zoom in on a specific mechanic, then zoom out to the business outcome.
- **Structure:** Lead with the philosophy (the machine), then break the motion into stages/steps (numbered, each with real depth), then the outcome.
- **Watch for:** Don't list deliverables like a spec sheet. Frame each capability as "here's the problem it solves and how it moves the account."
- **Example opener:**
  > Most agencies "run your LinkedIn ads." We build the system around them. Paid is the amplifier — but it only compounds when it sits on top of organic authority, warm-audience retargeting, and account-level signals telling you who's actually in-market. Here's how the motion works:

### 8.3 Product page — DemandSense (or any product layer)
- **Job:** Make the abstract concrete. Signals/intelligence copy drifts vague fast — anchor it in what the user *sees and does*.
- **Register:** Specific and practical. Solutions and outcomes over feature lists. (Internal note from the team: DS copy should be outcome-based, not feature-based.)
- **Structure:** The problem in the reader's words → what DemandSense shows you → what you do with it → the pipeline result.
- **Example:**
  > You're running ads to accounts you can't see. DemandSense identifies the companies engaging with your content and visiting your site — ICP-fit, ad-exposed, and showing intent — so you stop guessing which accounts are warming up and start acting on it while they're still paying attention.

### 8.4 About / Story
- **Job:** This is where Justin is most human — and the highest-trust page. Use the real story.
- **Register:** Warmest calibration. A little more personality allowed (still clean). Self-aware, not self-important.
- **Structure:** The origin (restaurants → LinkedIn organic → agency → acquired → building toward scale), the conviction that came out of it, and why that makes Impactable different.
- **Watch for:** No hero-founder mythology. Keep the self-deprecating honesty that makes it real. Every claim must be true (see Section 10).
- **Example opener:**
  > Impactable didn't start in a marketing boardroom. It started with LinkedIn organic — using it to build a business, become part-owner of a restaurant franchise, then a side hustle, then an agency. That's where the conviction comes from: LinkedIn works when you treat it as a system, not a slot machine. We've now run 500+ B2B campaigns on that belief.

### 8.5 Proof / Case studies / Results
- **Job:** Show the work. This is Justin's home turf — specificity is the whole point.
- **Register:** Let the numbers and the mechanism carry it. Minimal adjectives.
- **Structure:** The account's real situation → what was broken/missed → what we changed → the specific result → the principle it proves.
- **Watch for:** Never round up or invent. If a number wasn't given, don't manufacture one (Section 10). "Account progression" framing beats a single last-touch stat.

### 8.6 Pricing
- **Job:** Reduce friction, be straight. Justin hates opacity and games.
- **Register:** Plain, honest, no pressure. Explain what the buyer gets and why it's structured that way.
- **Watch for:** No fake urgency, no "limited time." If the model is custom, say why (it scales with their budget and goals) rather than hiding it.

### 8.7 CTA blocks (site-wide)
- **Register:** Direct, low-pressure, specific about what happens next.
- **Language bank:** "Book a strategy call." "Let's talk." "See what we'd change in your account." "Get a read on your setup." — Avoid "Get started today!", "Unlock your free…", "Don't miss out."

### 8.8 FAQ / Objection handling
- **Job:** This is where the contrarian, empathy-before-correction voice shines. Each answer = acknowledge the real concern, then reframe.
- **Example:**
  > **"Isn't LinkedIn Ads just expensive?"**
  > It looks that way when it's run in isolation and judged on last-touch form fills. Per-click, LinkedIn costs more than search. But that's the wrong scoreboard. Run as part of a full-funnel system, it's building account awareness and pipeline that shows up across every other channel you run. We measure it on account progression, not just the last form.

## 9. Structure & scannability rules (all pages)

- Front-load the value. The most important idea goes in the first line of any section — don't bury it.
- One idea per section. Don't stack three points into one paragraph.
- Short paragraphs, generous white space, purposeful line breaks.
- Lists earn depth: each item is a real, useful thought — not a one-word bullet, not padded filler.
- End sections on a landing line, not a trailing thought. Justin lands hard; no flowery wrap-ups.
- No "conclusion paragraph that restates everything above."

---

## 10. Proof & specificity rules (non-negotiable)

- **Never fabricate.** No invented client names, revenue figures, team sizes, campaign counts, percentages, or logos. If a specific number isn't supplied for a given page, either request it or write around it without inventing.
- **Prefer real, verifiable specifics** the business owns: 500+ campaigns, 100+ active B2B clients, LinkedIn Marketing Partner status, DemandSense capabilities. Confirm any stat before it goes on a page.
- **Anonymize** client scenarios unless there's explicit permission to name them.
- **Replace vague claims with mechanisms.** Instead of "amazing ROI," explain *how* the result happens (account progression, warm retargeting, signal-based targeting).
- When in doubt on a fact, flag it in a comment for Justin rather than guessing.

---

## 11. Quick reference — the "sounds like Justin on the web" gut check

It's working when the copy:
- Teaches something specific a marketer or CEO could act on.
- Takes a clear stance and names the lazy default it's arguing against.
- Uses real numbers and named tools/channels, no vague hype.
- Reads clean and scannable — but still has an uneven, human cadence and a spine.
- Connects the tactic to the business outcome (pipeline/revenue), not just impressions.
- Sounds like a sharp operator talking straight — not a brochure, not a LinkedIn screenshot.

## 12. Pre-publish QA gate

Before shipping any page, confirm every item is TRUE:

**Voice**
- [ ] Reads like cleaned-up Justin: direct, specific, confident — not corporate, not raw-LinkedIn.
- [ ] Rawness is calibrated for web (Section 2): no typos, ≤1 ellipsis, no slang, minimal/zero ALL CAPS.
- [ ] Directness, specificity, and contrarian stance are at FULL strength (not softened).

**Substance**
- [ ] Value-first: strip the personality and there's still something useful.
- [ ] At least one specific number, named tool, or concrete mechanism per major section.
- [ ] Connects to a business outcome (pipeline/revenue), not vanity metrics.
- [ ] Reinforces at least one core positioning conviction (Section 6).

**Mechanics**
- [ ] Headline promises value or reframes a belief; subhead delivers on it.
- [ ] Uneven, human sentence rhythm; scannable; white space; lists have depth.
- [ ] No banned words (Section 5). No hedging in front of confident claims.
- [ ] Lands on a strong line; no restate-everything conclusion.

**Integrity**
- [ ] Zero fabricated facts, numbers, names, or logos (Section 10).
- [ ] Any uncertain stat is flagged for Justin, not guessed.

**Pairing**
- [ ] Visual/design/brand decisions handed to the `impactable-brand` skill — not faked in copy.
